Machine Learning in Mechanical Engineering

Optimizing Traditional Manufacturing Processes Like Thread Cutting

Komal Krishna

April 28, 2025 • 8 min read

When I first started my journey in Mechanical Engineering, I was deeply fascinated by machines, materials, and how precision plays a crucial role in manufacturing. But as I explored more, I found myself equally drawn to the potential of technology—especially Machine Learning (ML)—in revolutionizing these conventional processes.

During my final year, I had the opportunity to work on a major project that focused on integrating Machine Learning into thread cutting operations. It was an eye-opener that changed the way I saw manufacturing. Traditionally, processes like thread cutting depend heavily on fixed parameters, operator experience, and trial-and-error methods. But what if we could make the system intelligent enough to learn from data and optimize itself? That was the question that drove our project.

Understanding the Problem

Thread cutting requires extreme precision. Factors like tool wear, material hardness, spindle speed, feed rate, and cutting force can greatly affect the final quality. In traditional methods, identifying the optimal combination of these factors involves manual calculations or costly physical trials.

We wanted to shift from this guess-based approach to a data-driven model.

How Machine Learning Helped

Using a dataset generated from multiple thread cutting trials, we trained a Machine Learning model (we experimented with different algorithms but focused more on explainability and performance). The idea was to predict output parameters like surface finish and thread accuracy based on the input cutting conditions.

We cleaned and processed the data, extracted features, and trained our model to learn patterns between inputs (like cutting speed and tool geometry) and outputs (like thread depth, burr formation, or surface roughness). Over time, our model was able to suggest the most optimal cutting conditions that reduced material waste and improved precision.

CNC machine used for thread cutting operations

Data analysis and model training process

Key Benefits Observed

  • Improved Efficiency: ML helped us find optimal cutting conditions faster than manual methods.
  • Predictive Maintenance: By analyzing tool wear patterns, we could predict when a tool needed replacing—reducing downtime.
  • Higher Accuracy: The model's predictions helped in achieving better dimensional accuracy with fewer reworks.
  • Scalability: Once trained, the model could be used for other similar manufacturing processes with minimal changes.

What I Learned

This project wasn't just about implementing a model; it was about bridging the gap between traditional mechanical engineering and modern computational intelligence. It taught me how powerful ML can be when used to optimize processes that were once entirely manual. It also gave me a new direction for my career—one that combines my mechanical foundation with a growing passion for computer science.

Final Thoughts

As someone coming from a mechanical background, I truly believe that the future of manufacturing lies in interdisciplinary collaboration. Machine Learning isn't here to replace engineers—it's here to empower us to work smarter.

If you're a mechanical engineer curious about ML, start small—learn about data, try a few simple models, and most importantly, look at your everyday processes through a data-driven lens. You'll be surprised at how much potential there is waiting to be unlocked.
