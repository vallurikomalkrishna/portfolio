Transitioning from Engineering to Tech

Komal Krishna

February 28, 2025 • 6 min read

When I started my journey in mechanical engineering, I never imagined I'd one day be building websites and writing code for a living. Like many, I chose engineering with a mix of curiosity and societal expectation. But somewhere along the way, I realized my interests were shifting toward the world of tech — especially web development.

During my third year, I started exploring tech-related topics out of pure interest. I built my first basic website and something clicked. It felt good — not just seeing the final output, but also the problem-solving process behind it. Slowly, I found myself spending more time learning HTML, CSS, JavaScript, and even exploring frameworks like React. What started as curiosity turned into a passion.

At first, the transition wasn't easy. I had to balance my engineering coursework with self-learning. I had no formal CS background, so I relied on online resources, YouTube, and documentation.

My real push into tech came when I got the opportunity to work on the TEDxAnuragU website. One of my lecturers encouraged me to take it up, and that small push made a big impact. I was nervous, but excited — and by the time the website went live, I knew I wanted to do more of this.

Shortly after, I joined the IUCEE Student Chapter at Anurag University. That was another milestone in my journey. Working with the chapter exposed me to new challenges and helped me understand the importance of technology in problem-solving. It wasn't just web development anymore — I started getting curious about cloud technologies, deployment, backend systems, and how everything works together.

Every small project I worked on added up. I took freelance gigs, built websites for friends, and contributed to events. Eventually, I stareted freelancing, where we developed websites and digital solutions for real clients. Each project brought in new learnings — not just technical, but also communication, client handling, and time management.

In this process, I even started working on machine learning projects out of pure interest. From predicting soil fertility to exploring emotion detection using speech, I dove into areas I never thought I’d explore as a mechanical engineering student. These projects helped me understand the practical power of AI and how it can be integrated with real-world applications — especially when combined with web interfaces and cloud services.

One of the biggest highlights of my journey was my final year major project, where I combined machine learning with manufacturing. We built a solution that integrated ML algorithms to improve predictive analytics in precision machining for thread-cutting operations. The project not only pushed me technically, but it also received great recognition from the department and external reviewers for its innovation and relevance to real-world industry problems.

What I Learned Along the Way

  • Start Before You Feel Ready: Taking up the TEDx website was a big step, and I'm glad I didn't wait to feel "prepared."
  • Be Consistent: Self-learning isn't easy, but regular effort pays off.
  • Projects > Theory: Every real project taught me more than any lecture could.
  • Support Matters: That one lecturer's and the friend's support played a huge role in shaping my path.
  • Background Isn't a Barrier: Mechanical engineering taught me logic and discipline — and I carried that mindset into tech.

Today, I'm confident in what I do — whether it's building full-stack web applications, working with cloud platforms, or mentoring others starting out in tech. If you're from a non-CS background and thinking about switching, my advice is: don't wait. Just start. Stay curious, build projects, and you'll be surprised how far you can go.

Because sometimes, all it takes is one opportunity… and the rest follows.
